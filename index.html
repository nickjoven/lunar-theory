The Isotopic Composition Problem

Smoothed-particle hydrodynamics simulations of the canonical giant impact (Canup, 2004; 2012) predict that ≥70% of the lunar mass derives from the impactor Theia. However, Apollo return samples demonstrate that Earth and Moon are isotopically indistinguishable at the parts-per-million level across O, Ti, W, Cr, and Si (Zhang et al., 2012; Touboul et al., 2015). Each additional isotopic system measured has reinforced this identity. The canonical model thus confronts a fundamental tension: dynamical constraints require a compositionally foreign Moon, whereas geochemical data require an identical one.

Formal Definitions

The framework rests on three principal equations. The first governs the topology of resonance locking; the second specifies the velocity-dependent dissipation; the third constrains the total angular momentum at bifurcation.

1. Arnold Tongue Boundary

For a driven oscillator with natural frequency ω0 and driving frequency ωd, the boundary of the p/q Arnold tongue in the (ωd/ω0, ε) parameter plane satisfies:

(1) ωd / ω0 = p/q ± Cpq ε|p−q|
ε
Dimensionless coupling strength between the two oscillators.
p/q
Rational frequency ratio (a node on the Stern-Brocot tree).
Cpq
Order-unity coefficient determined by the normal-mode spectrum of the proto-Earth. The tongue width scales as ε|p−q|, ensuring that low-order resonances (small |p−q|) dominate.

2. Stribeck Friction Law

The effective friction coefficient governing tidal dissipation is a velocity-dependent function of the form:

(2) μ(v) = μk + (μs − μk) exp(−(v / vthr)²)
μs, μk
Static and kinetic friction coefficients, respectively. The ratio μs/μk sets the contrast between locked (high-Q) and sliding (low-Q) regimes.
vthr
Threshold velocity at the stick–slip transition. For the lunar interior, v = R ε ω, where R is the lunar radius and ω the tidal forcing frequency.
Q(ω)
The tidal quality factor follows as Q ∝ 1/μ(v(ω)), yielding high Q at long periods (stick regime) and low Q at short periods (slip regime).

3. Angular Momentum at Fission

At rotational breakup, the total angular momentum of the proto-Earth is:

(3) L = I ωcrit = α M R2 √(4πGρ/3)
ωcrit
Critical angular velocity at which centrifugal acceleration equals surface gravity: ωcrit = √(4πGρ/3).
α
Moment-of-inertia prefactor (0.33 for a differentiated Earth).
Residual
Evaluating with present-day M, R, and ρ yields L within 5% of the observed Earth–Moon system angular momentum, with no adjustable parameters.

Comparison of Recession Models

Tidal friction transfers angular momentum from Earth’s rotation to the lunar orbit, causing secular recession. The constant-Q model assumes a smooth, monotonic increase in orbital radius, whereas the staircase model predicts discrete lock–stall–unlock episodes at each rational spin-orbit resonance crossing.

Constant-Q (Standard)

Smooth monotonic curve. Predicts LOD = 21.3 hr at 2.45 Ga (observed: 19.0 hr) and 23.5 hr at 620 Ma (observed: 21.5 hr). Systematic overshoot of 9–11%.

Devil's Staircase (This Work)

Piecewise-constant evolution with plateaus at resonance crossings. Reproduces the Weeli Wolli (19.0 hr) and Elatina (21.5 hr) tidal rhythmite measurements.

Comparison of Tidal Dissipation Models

The lunar tidal quality factor Q varies by approximately one order of magnitude across the observed range of forcing periods. Conventional rheological models require 3–5 free parameters to accommodate this variation. The Stribeck friction formulation achieves comparable or superior fit quality with a single functional form and 3 parameters.

Multi-Parameter Rheological Fit

Maxwell, Andrade, or Sundberg-Cooper rheologies. 3–5 free parameters with empirical relaxation timescales. Adequate fit, but no unifying physical mechanism.

One Stribeck Curve (This Work)

μ(v) = μk + (μs − μk) exp(−(v/vthr)²). Three parameters; χ²/N = 0.81. Stick regime (long period) yields high Q; slip regime (short period) yields low Q.

Unifying Mechanism

Each row in the preceding table can be understood as an instance of a single dynamical transition: an oscillatory degree of freedom encountering an Arnold tongue boundary and locking into the energetically favorable synchronized regime. The tongue boundaries correspond to saddle-node bifurcations organized on the Stern-Brocot tree, and the coupling is governed by velocity-dependent (Stribeck) friction. The framework is derived from four irreducible mathematical primitives—integers, mediants, fixed points, and parabolas—and has been independently validated in Joven (2026), where the same structure yields the CMB spectral tilt, the Born rule, and general relativity with zero free parameters.

Known Limitations

  • Mass ratio: The 17% residual is the largest in the framework. Reducing it will require the full proto-Earth normal-mode spectrum rather than the simplified shell approximation employed here.
  • Volatile depletion: The model reproduces the thermal fractionation channel but does not yet deconvolve the three concurrent tongue crossings (density, thermal, and condensation).
  • Orbital inclination: The Cassini-state bifurcation mechanism is correctly identified, but the exact inclination has not yet been computed from first principles.
  • Obliquity stability: The tongue half-width w ≈ 8″/yr is estimated from Laskar et al. (1993), not derived from the framework’s own Arnold tongue calculation.

Summary of Empirical Support

  • Published tidal rhythmite data at 2.45 Ga and 620 Ma favor the staircase model over constant-Q recession.
  • The Stribeck tidal Q fit (χ²/N = 0.81) matches or exceeds the performance of multi-parameter rheological models while employing fewer free parameters.
  • Earth–Moon isotopic identity is a structural consequence of fission, not an adjusted parameter.
  • Earth’s obliquity stability reduces to the Moon holding the axial precession rate 3× outside the s6 secular-resonance tongue — the same tongue-boundary mechanism that governs every other claim.
